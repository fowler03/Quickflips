Spiritual Care Documentation

Sometimes spiritual care counselors have difficulty describing the problems and goals of their patients and families.  This results in documentation of “support” and “presence,” which may not accurately describe the professional interventions provided by this discipline.  These are some sample problems and interventions for spiritual care counselors:

 

Spiritual issues, as evidenced by:
  • No local church
  • Unresolved guilt
  • Fear/anxiety
  • Alienation from belief system
  • Search for meaning
  • Desire for peace
  • Patient or caregiver request for ritual or prayer
  • Spiritual crisis
 Interventions include:
  • Assess spiritual needs and preferences
  • Facilitate articulation of spiritual concerns
  • Provide non-judgmental listening
  • Assist in life review
  • Assist patient in composing an ethical will
  • Provide prayer, meditation, scripture reading
  • Facilitate relaxation response
  • Provide opportunities for trust-building
  • Facilitate linkage with local faith community
  • Assist to identify and process feelings related to illness, death and dying
  • Provide education re: spiritual dynamics at end-of-life
  • Assist in identifying spiritual strengths and challenges
  • Assist in identifying what patient/family’s faith and faith background has taught them re:  afterlife/burial/ cremation/redemption/ absolution/forgiveness
 Final arrangements, as evidenced by:
  • No arrangements made
  • Request for information
  • Request for assistance
 Interventions include:
  • Discuss final arrangements with patient/family
  • Assist with making arrangements
  • Plan to conduct funeral or memorial service
 The attending physician is identified by the patient upon admission as having the most significant role in the determination and delivery of his/her medical care.  The attending physician:
  • Consents to hospice care
  • Certifies the patient’s terminal prognosis
  • Signs subsequent medical orders

 

It is important that you help the patient accurately identify who his/her attending will be at the time the patient elects hospice.  One of the hospice patient’s rights is to choose his or her attending physician.  The patient’s choice of attending physician should be documented. The referring physician is not necessarily the attending physician.  The physician who signs the initial certification of terminal illness should be the attending physician.  Having one doctor sign the COTI and another doctor listed as attending is incorrect.

 

In addition to explaining the role of attending physician to the patient/family, there are questions you can ask to determine who the attending will be.

  • Questions to ask the patient (or his/her representative) in order to get this information:
  • Who is your “regular” doctor?
  • Which doctor would you like to visit regularly, now that you are a hospice patient?
  • Questions to ask the referring physician:
  • Will Dr. Referent sign the Plan of Care and continue to follow this patient?
  • Would Dr. Referent like to see this patient after admission to hospice?
  • Is Dr. Referent this patient’s primary care physician?
