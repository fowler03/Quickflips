ALS

Two factors are critical in determining prognosis:  ability to breathe and, to a lesser extent, ability to swallow.  Artificial ventilation or feeding will significantly alter six-month prognosis.  Examination by a neurologist within three months of assessment for hospice is advised.

 

Patients will be considered to be in the terminal stage of ALS (life expectancy of six months or less) if they meet the following criteria (must fulfill 1 and/or 2):
  1. Critically impaired breathing capacity. 
    1. Vital capacity (VC) less than 40% of predicted (seated or supine), and two or more of the following signs:
    • Dyspnea at rest
    • Orthopnea
    • Paradoxical abdominal motion
    • Respiratory rate > 20 per minute
    • Reduced speech/vocal volume
    • Weakened Cough
    • Symptoms of sleep disordered breathing
    • Frequent awakening
    • Daytime somnolence, excessive sleepiness in daytime
    • Unexplained headaches
    • Unexplained confusion
    • Unexplained anxiety
    • Unexplained nausea
    • Use of accessory respiratory musculature
    • If unable to perform the FVC test, patients may be eligible if three or more of the above symptoms are documented.
    • Not all patients can or will undertake regular pulmonary function tests
  2. Severe nutritional insufficiency, defined as:
    1. Dysphagia with progressive weight loss of at least five percent of body weight with or without election of feeding tube
