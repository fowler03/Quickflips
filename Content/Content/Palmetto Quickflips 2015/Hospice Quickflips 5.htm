Cardiopulmonary Conditions

This LCD presents a framework for identifying, documenting, and communicating the unique health care needs of individuals with cardiopulmonary (CP) conditions.

Cardiopulmonary (CP) conditions are associated with impairments, activity limitations, and disability.  Their impact depends on the individual’s over-all health status.  CP conditions may support a prognosis of six months or less under many clinical scenarios.

 

The basis for palliative interventions and care-planning is your documentation of the items below:

  1. Identification of specific structural/functional impairments
  2. Relevant activity limitations
  3. Use of the International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (ICF) to identify and document service needs of individuals with CP conditions is suggested, but not required.  (See ICF Worksheet.)  The following are components that need to be addressed in your documentation:
  1. Appetite
  2. Sleep functions such as amount and quality of sleep
  3. Pain in the chest
  4. Heart functions such as heart rhythm, contraction force of ventricular muscles and blood supply to the heart
  5. General physical endurance
  6. Sensations related to the disease such as tightness of the chest and dyspnea
  7. Structural damage such as atria or aortic valve
  8. Walking and moving around using transportation
  9. Particular interpersonal interactions and relationships such as informal social relationships with friends and socializing for recreation and leisure
  10. Assistive devices and technology used for mobility, indoors and outdoors, and transportation
  11. Support systems from immediate families
  12. Health systems in place

 

The care plan may be impacted by secondary and comorbid conditions.  The clinician will need to identify and document relevant secondary and comorbid conditions, together with the above mentioned items to help establish hospice eligibility and to maintain a beneficiary-centered plan of care.

 

Secondary and Comorbid Conditions – The significance of a given comorbid condition is best described by defining the structural/functional impairments, together with any limitation in activity and restriction in participation, related to the comorbid condition.  For example, a beneficiary with a primary or cardiopulmonary condition and ESRD could have specific ESRD-related impairments of water, mineral and electrolyte balance functions coexisting with the cardiopulmonary impairments associated with the primary cardiopulmonary condition (e.g., Aortic Stenosis, COPD or Heart Failure).

 

Ultimately, in order to support a hospice plan of care, the combined effects of the primary cardiopulmonary condition and any identified comorbid condition(s) should be such that most beneficiaries with the identified impairments would have a prognosis of six months or less.
