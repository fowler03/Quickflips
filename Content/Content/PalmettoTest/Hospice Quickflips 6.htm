This LCD presents a framework for identifying, documenting, and communicating the unique health care needs of individuals with neurological conditions.

Neurological conditions are associated with impairments, activity limitations, and disability.  Their impact depends on the individual’s over-all health status.  Neurological conditions may support a prognosis of six months or less under many clinical scenarios.

The basis for palliative interventions and care-planning is your documentation of the items below:

  1. Identification of specific structural/functional impairments
  2. Relevant activity limitations
  3. Use of the International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (ICF) to identify and document service needs of individuals with neurological conditions is suggested, but not required.  The following are some components that should be addressed in your documentation:
  1. Structures of the nervous system
  2. Mental function
  3. Sensory functions and pain
  4. Neuromusculoskeletal and movement functions
  5. Communication
  6. Mobility issues
  7. Self-care ability

The care plan may be impacted by secondary and comorbid conditions.  The clinician will need to identify and document relevant secondary and comorbid conditions, together with the above mentioned items to help establish hospice eligibility and to maintain a beneficiary-centered plan of care.

 

Secondary and Comorbid Conditions – The significance of a given comorbid condition is best described by defining the structural/functional impairments, together with any limitation in activity and restriction in participation, related to the comorbid condition.  For example, a beneficiary with a primary neurological condition such as ALS and a comorbidity of COPD could have specific COPD-related structural and functional impairments of respiration (e.g., structural impairments of the brochoalveolar tree resulting in increased respiratory rate, cough and impaired gas exchange) that contribute to the activity limitations and participation restrictions already present due to the respiratory muscle weakness often observed with ALS.

Such a combination could affect the palliative care plan by contributing to the individual’s dyspnea and impaired exercise tolerance.  Further description/documentation using the activities and participation component of the ICF (e.g., mobility, self-care, and interpersonal interactions and relationships), would help complete the clinical picture.  Palliative care aimed at relieving the dyspnea and improving the individual’s health status would be the goal.

The adult failure to thrive syndrome is characterized by unexplained weight loss, malnutrition and disability.  The syndrome has been associated with multiple primary conditions (e.g., infections and malignancies), but always includes two defining clinical elements:

  • Nutrition impairment

AND

  • Disability

The nutritional impairment and disability associated with the adult failure to thrive syndrome may manifest as an irreversible progression in the patient’s nutritional impairment/disability despite a trial of therapy (i.e., treatment intended to affect the primary condition responsible for the patient’s clinical presentation).

The presence of comorbid conditions may hasten the patient’s clinical progression and as such should be identified and addressed.

This hospice policy addresses those cases where reversible causes of severe nutritional impairment and disability (i.e., the adult failure to thrive syndrome) have been excluded.

 

Medical criteria 1 and 2 are important indicators of nutritional and functional status respectively, and would thus support a terminal prognosis if met.

  1. The nutritional impairment associated with the adult failure to thrive syndrome should be severe enough to impact on a beneficiary’s weight.  It is expected that the Body Mass Index (BMI) of beneficiaries electing the Medicare Hospice Benefit for the adult failure to thrive syndrome will be below 22 kg/m2 and that the patient is either declining enteral/parenteral nutritional support or has not responded to such nutritional support, despite an adequate caloric intake.


BMI (kg/m2) = 703 x (weight in pounds) ÷ (height in inches)2

 

  1. The disability associated with the adult failure to thrive syndrome should be such that the individual is significantly disabled. Significant disability would be demonstrated by a Karnofsky or Palliative Performance Scale value less than or equal to 40%.


The following must be documented:

  • A current BMI determined using the beneficiary’s height and weight measured
  • Within six months (180 days) of the most recent certification/recertification date for beneficiaries without enteral nutritional support

Or

  • At the time of initial certification and at each subsequent recertification for beneficiaries receiving enteral nutritional support
  • If recumbent anthropometry (i.e., mid-arm circumference in cm2 is substituted for BMI at recertification, the rationale should be documented
  • A current evaluation of the beneficiary’s functional status demonstrating a level of disability equivalent to that described by a Karnofsky or Palliative Performance Scale value of less than or equal to 40%, determined:
  • Within six months (180 days) from the most recent certification/recertification date for beneficiaries with enteral nutritional support

Or

  • At the time of initial certification and at each subsequent recertification for beneficiaries receiving enteral nutritional support

 

Abnormal weight loss and cachexia are additional secondary diagnoses which may describe the clinical circumstances.

 

 

Note: Adult Failure to Thrive and Debility cannot be used as the primary hospice diagnosis.

This LCD presents a framework for identifying, documenting, and communication the unique health care needs of individuals with end stage renal disease (ESRD).

ESRD may support a diagnosis of six months or less under many different clinical scenarios.  The structural and functional impairments associated with a primary diagnosis of ESRD are often complicated by comorbid and/or secondary conditions.

The basis for palliative interventions and care-planning is your documentation of the following items:

  1. Identification of specific structural/functional impairments
  2. Relevant activity limitations
  3. Use of the International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (ICF) to identify and document service needs of individuals with ESRD is suggested, but not required.  The following are some components that may be addressed in your documentation (list and examples not all inclusive):
  1. Structures of the nervous system
  2. Structures of the cardiovascular and respiratory system e.g. uremic pericarditis, coronary artery disease, peripheral vascular disease
  3. Structure related to the digestive, metabolic and endocrine systems such as structure of stomach due to PEG tube
  4. Structure related to genitourinary and reproductive system such as structure of kidneys due to nephrosclerosis
  5. Mental  function such as state of consciousness, orientation
  6. Functions of the cardiovascular, hematological, immunological and respiratory system, e.g. sepsis, immunosuppression/AIDS, albumin <3.5 gm/dl, cachexia, platelet count< 25,000, disseminated intravascular coagulation
  7. Functions of the digestive, metabolic and endocrine systems e.g. GI bleed, secondary hyperparathyroidism, anorexia, swallowing problems, regurgitation and vomiting as evidence by aspiration pneumonia
  8. Genitourinary and reproductive functions such as urinary excretory functions as evidenced by abnormal creatinine clearance and/or serum creatinine, intractable hyperkalemia
  9. Neuromusculoskeletal and movement related functions
  10. Mobility such as changing basic body position, walking
  11. Self Care, e.g. washing oneself, toileting, dressing
  12. Environmental factors such as assistive devices  used,  family support, decision not to seek dialysis or renal transplant

The care plan may be impacted by secondary and comorbid conditions.  The clinician will need to identify and document relevant secondary and comorbid conditions, together with the above mentioned items to help establish hospice eligibility and to maintain a beneficiary-centered plan of care.

Secondary and Comorbid Conditions – The significance of a given comorbid condition is best described by defining the structural/functional impairments, together with any limitation in activity, related to the comorbid condition.  For example, a beneficiary with ESRD and clinically significant CHD would have specific impairments of cardiovascular structure/function (e.g., narrowing of coronary arteries, dyspnea, orthopnea, chest pain) which may or may not respond/be amenable to treatment.  The identified impairments in cardiovascular structure/function may be associated with activity limitations (e.g., mobility, self-care).  Ultimately, the combined effects of the ESRD and any comorbid condition should be such that most beneficiaries with ESRD and similar impairments would have a prognosis of six months or less.

Stage 7 on the Reisberg Functional Assessment Staging (FAST) Scale identifies the threshold of activity limitation that would support a six month prognosis.  The FAST Scale does not address the impact of comorbid and secondary conditions.  These are thus considered separately.

Stage #7: Loss of speech, locomotion and consciousness

  • Sub-stage 7a:  Ability to speak limited (1 to 5 words a day)
  • Sub-stage 7b:  All intelligible vocabulary lost
  • Sub-stage 7c:  Non-ambulatory
  • Sub-stage 7d:  Unable to sit up independently
  • Sub-stage 7e:  Unable to smile
  • Sub-stage 7f:  Unable to hold head up

Comorbid Conditions:

The significance of a given comorbid condition is best described by defining the structural and functional impairments related to the comorbid condition, together with any limitation in activity.

The comprehensive assessment and Plan of Care (POC) are mirrors of each other.  The POC must be individualized, based on the initial and ongoing comprehensive assessment of the patient.  Assessment and care planning are ongoing, continuous processes updated by the interdisciplinary team (IDT), as the patient’s needs and condition changes.  Use the following lists to ensure that your assessment and POC are complete.

Assessment should include:

  • Physical, psychosocial, emotional and spiritual needs
  • Nature and condition of terminal illness
  • Complications and risk factors
  • Functional status
  • Severity of symptoms
  • Patient response to hospice interventions
  • Drug profile
  • Bereavement needs
  • Imminence of death
  • Referrals
  • Data elements that allow for measurement of outcomes

Plan of Care should include:

  • Patient and family goals
  • Interventions for problems identified through assessment process
  • All services for palliation and management of terminal illness and related conditions
  • Drugs and treatments
  • Scope and frequency of services
  • Medical supplies and appliances
  •  
  • Measurable outcomes
  • Coordination with other providers
  • Documentation of patient/family understanding and agreement with the POC
  • Progress towards outcomes and goals

 

Hospice Item Set (HIS) and Impact on Assessment and Care Planning

  • Beginning July 2014, CMS mandated that hospices report quality data utilizing the Hospice Item Set (HIS) as the data collection tool
  • Seven (7) quality measures  chosen by CMS  are seen as “best practices” which are a reflection of the level of quality care provided
  • HIS data will be pulled from the IDT’s assessments and interventions in the POC
  • Timeliness in the completion of assessment and POC is essential to meet the CMS mandated timeframes (See Quickflip #16 for Hospice Dates and Times)
  • See Quickflip #13a for Hospice Item Set

Follow these steps for a dynamic, continuous assessment and care planning process.

  1. Patients are assessed by each discipline.  Problems, goals and interventions are identified (based on the patient’s/family’s needs and goals).  This group of individualized problems/goals/interventions is collectively called the Plan of Care (POC).
  2. Based on the POC, each discipline provides care/service to the patient/family.  That means that each visit should be focused on a problem identified on the POC, and reflect professional intervention(s) to achieve the desired goal.
  3. Document the patient’s progress toward meeting the desired goal(s) in the POC after each visit.  The Interdisciplinary Team (IDT) summarizes the effectiveness of interventions and plans for the next two weeks on the IDT summary form.  Every visit note and IDT note should reflect attention to the problems/goals/interventions.
  4. If you are seeing a patient, and the focus of the visit is not on one of the problems identified in the POC, ask “Am I focused on a new problem?”  If so, add it to the POC!  If you are not focused on a new or existing problem, ask “Is there a need for me to visit?”
  5. If you are seeing a patient and none of the problems on the POC requires you or the patient’s attention, ask yourself, “Are these problems resolved?”  If so, document the resolution.
  6. If there are consistently no problems that you and the patient/family can work to resolve together, ask yourself “Am I seeing this patient too frequently?  Does this patient require intervention from my discipline at this time?”  If you are a case manager, ask “Is this patient still eligible for hospice care?”
  7. Adjust visit frequencies for the upcoming two weeks based on patient/family needs and goals.

The Hospice Item Set (HIS) has been initiated by CMS to gather data on the use of best practices in assessments and the initiation of specific interventions in hospice.  This data will be obtained by each hospice by pulling information from all admission and discharge documentation.  The HIS has specific questions to provide data on seven (7) quality measures.

 

The HIS is not an assessment tool, but it is important for clinicians to understand how their documentation will be used in quality reporting.  The measures selected have already been approved by the National Quality Forum (NQF) and are intended to show that a specific hospice “Does the right things, right.”

 

The quality measures are:

  • NQF #1641 – Treatment Preferences  (Discussions regarding advanced directives)
  • NQF (modified) #1647 – Beliefs/Values Addressed
  • NQF #1634 – Pain Screening
  • NQF #1637 – Pain Assessment
  • NQF #1639 – Dyspnea Screening
  • NQF #1638 – Dyspnea Treatment
  • NQF #1617 – Patients Treated with an Opioid who are Given a Bowel Regimen

 

As a part of the team caring for the hospice patient, your assessment always determines the POC.  Your assessments and discussions put patient interventions into motion.  The documentation of assessments, discussions and your interventions must be complete in order to provide accurate data for the HIS.  Be sure to take credit by providing thorough and timely documentation.

Quick Tips:

  • Who does the patient/family really need to see?  If the issue is spiritual pain, don’t increase nursing visits — refer to the appropriate IDT members, including volunteers.
  • A lack of a caregiver does not necessarily mean that nursing visits will increase.  In some cases, this only serves to keep the patient in an unsafe environment.  Be sure to refer all patients who do not have a caregiver to the social worker.
  • Use the phone — ask questions to determine what needs the patient/family has prior to scheduling a visit.
  • Remember to document the reason for any missed visits, notify the interdisciplinary team and physician, and update the Plan of Care if visit frequency needs to change.

No visits:

  • Patient/family refuse services, have no goals or needs they think require intervention.   When this occurs, do a monthly phone check if this is acceptable to the patient/family.
  • Provide support and education to other team members in handling psychosocial and spiritual issues.  Call family if it sounds as though such issues are escalating.

Monthly visits with a phone check in between:

  • Patient/family has a support system of friends, relatives and/or clergy.
  • Final arrangements, advance directives and resources are all currently adequate for patient and families goals and needs.
  • Patient/family decline services, but have needs as assessed by the team.  Visit monthly if they will allow it, to assess and attempt to facilitate an alliance.

Two times/month:

  • Patient/family have support, but there are issues they feel more comfortable addressing with someone outside that support system
  • Linkage with resources that take time to process

Weekly visits with a phone check:

  • Intense emotional distress, weak support system (refer to a mental health professional if psychotherapy is indicated)
  • Ongoing resource linkage that takes a shorter time to process

Two times/week:

  • Intense emotional distress, weak support system (refer to a mental health professional if psychotherapy is indicated)
  • Crisis intervention or safety concerns that require continuous assessment and intervention until resolved
  • Linkage with resources requiring intensive efforts and a rapid timeline

 

Daily visits:

  • Crisis intervention or safety issues that require daily visits until resolved
  • Linkage with community resources that are urgent and have a rapid timeline

Quick Tips:

  • Spiritual care counselors and social workers do not visit for social support only; refer to a volunteer.
  • Frequent visits for crisis intervention or safety should be time-limited.  If the issue is not resolved quickly, referral, placement or a report to protective services may be appropriate.  Hospice employees cannot make an unsafe situation safe by visiting frequently.

 

90:  The first and second Medicare Hospice Benefit election periods are 90 days each.

60:  Subsequent unlimited election periods are 60 days each.

6:  Prognosis must be six months or less if the illness runs its normal course.

2 days (before the end of day 3):  After care is initiated, a certification of terminal illness (COTI) by the hospice medical director/physician AND the patient’s attending physician is required.  This may be in the form of a documented verbal certification.  When written, the hospice medical director OR attending physician must include a brief narrative explanation of the clinical findings that support a life expectancy of six months or less as part of the certification.

Within 2 days (before the end of day 3) of the recertification date, a signed or verbal COTI from the hospice medical director or hospice physician is required. When written, the hospice medical director must include a brief narrative explanation of the clinical findings that support a life expectancy of six months or less as part of the certification.

48 hours:  RN must complete the initial assessment.

5 calendar days:  IDT in consultation with attending physician (if any) completes the comprehensive assessment.

Before billing:  A signed and dated COTI (with physician narrative) for each certification period must be on file before billing [or earlier if required by state regulations]. 

15 days:  COTI is valid for up to 15 days before the beginning of each certification period.

Many social workers and spiritual care counselors say that it’s difficult for them to ask patients and families facing a terminal illness about goals. These are some ways to elicit this information in a way that is sensitive.

  • “Tell me about what you hope for in the coming days/weeks/months.”
  • “Are there things you have left to do?”
  • “What are some of your worries about the future?”
  • “What are some of the problems facing you now?”
  • “What are some of the things you wish you could talk about?”
  • “If you could be granted a wish (other than a physical cure) what would it be?”
  • “Are there things your family needs help with?”
  • “How can I help you the most?”

Sometimes spiritual care counselors have difficulty describing the problems and goals of their patients and families.  This results in documentation of “support” and “presence,” which may not accurately describe the professional interventions provided by this discipline.  These are some sample problems and interventions for spiritual care counselors:

Spiritual issues, as evidenced by:

  • No local church
  • Unresolved guilt
  • Fear/anxiety
  • Alienation from belief system
  • Search for meaning
  • Desire for peace
  • Patient or caregiver request for ritual or prayer
  • Spiritual crisis
Interventions include:
  • Assess spiritual needs and preferences
  • Facilitate articulation of spiritual concerns
  • Provide non-judgmental listening
  • Assist in life review
  • Assist patient in composing an ethical will
  • Provide prayer, meditation, scripture reading
  • Facilitate relaxation response
  • Provide opportunities for trust-building
  • Facilitate linkage with local faith community
  • Assist to identify and process feelings related to illness, death and dying
  • Provide education re: spiritual dynamics at end-of-life
  • Assist in identifying spiritual strengths and challenges
  • Assist in identifying what patient/family’s faith and faith background has taught them re:  afterlife/burial/ cremation/redemption/ absolution/forgiveness

The attending physician is identified by the patient upon admission as having the most significant role in the determination and delivery of his/her medical care.  The attending physician:

  • Consents to hospice care
  • Certifies the patient’s terminal prognosis
  • Signs subsequent medical orders

It is important that you help the patient accurately identify who his/her attending will be at the time the patient elects hospice.  One of the hospice patient’s rights is to choose his or her attending physician.  The patient’s choice of attending physician should be documented.  The referring physician is not necessarily the attending physician.  The physician who signs the initial certification of terminal illness should be the attending physician.  Having one doctor sign the COTI and another doctor listed as attending is incorrect.

In addition to explaining the role of attending physician to the patient/family, there are questions you can ask to determine who the attending will be.

  • Questions to ask the patient (or his/her representative) in order to get this information:
  • Who is your “regular” doctor?
  • Which doctor would you like to visit regularly, now that you are a hospice patient?
  • Questions to ask the referring physician:
  • Will Dr. Referent sign the Plan of Care and continue to follow this patient?
  • Would Dr. Referent like to see this patient after admission to hospice?
  • Is Dr. Referent this patient’s primary care physician?

 

The patient may continue to see the attending physician for care related to the hospice diagnosis.  The attending physician bills Medicare Part B for those visits.

Any other doctor the patient sees for care related to the hospice diagnosis cannot bill Medicare Part B; if he/she does, the bills will be denied.  If the patient wants to see another doctor for care related to the hospice diagnosis, the hospice has to approve it and pay the doctor directly.

 

A hospice may arrange for another individual or entity to furnish services to its hospice patients.  The hospice must have a legally binding written agreement for the provision of arranged services.

If the team decides a consultation is needed, ask your supervisor if an agreement or contract is in place for that physician. 

You must document:

  • That the decision to refer was discussed by the IDT
  • That the reason for referring is related to the hospice diagnosis and part of the Plan of Care
  • The results/outcome of the consulting physician visit, as shared by the consulting physician

The hospice pays the consulting physician, and then bills Medicare for reimbursement for professional services.  Any procedures performed (and included in the POC by the team) come out of the hospice daily rate.

This only applies if the consultation is related to the hospice diagnosis. 

If the patient wishes to see a doctor for a problem that is not related to the hospice diagnosis, he /she can see any doctor he/she wishes, and that doctor bills Medicare directly.

Please DO!

  • Come to IDT meeting knowing the care plan for all your patients, the patient’s response to the care plan interventions and whether any interventions need to be changed.
  • Talk to your teammates! Leave voicemail messages, e-mail, and read each other’s progress notes between IDT meetings.  Document that you communicated with them!  The meeting is not meant to take the place of interdisciplinary communication.
  • Gently redirect your colleagues if they go off on tangents during the meeting.
  • Use staff support and other “together” times to share your triumphs, tears and challenges.  That way you won’t feel “cut off” when redirected during IDT meeting.
  • Be concise and stick to the agreed upon presentation format when reporting on patients.  This leaves time for in-depth discussion of patients who require it.
  • Document “Well managed with current interventions” or “Well managed with the current Plan of Care” instead of “No change.”  This simply means that the patient is being comfortably maintained due to your interventions.  All your efforts and professional interventions are documented in your progress/visit notes.  Document that patient comfort is being maintained successfully with the current clinical regimen.

 

Please DO NOT!

  • Come to IDT meeting without knowing which problems you are working on with your patients.
  • Tell the story of your last visit.
  • Save all your communication for the IDT meeting.  It’s just one meeting, and it’s not intended to be a substitute for regular interdisciplinary communication and collaboration.
