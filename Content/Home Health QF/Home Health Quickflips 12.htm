Reasonable and Necessary Criteria for Therapy Services

 

Follow these principles to demonstrate that therapy services are reasonable and medically necessary.

Principle One: Document the complexity of the treatment

  • Use standardized, objective tests and measures (e.g., measure strength deficits on 0-5 scale, measure Range of Motion deficits in degrees, measure balance with a standardized tool such as the Tinetti Test)
  • Describe therapeutic techniques such as neuromotor facilitation, passive joint mobilization, static sitting balance challenges, etc.
  • Emphasize teaching and training activities and the patient’s ability to follow through
  • Document progress in objective terms that demonstrate skills of the therapist:
  • Example:  Instead of “ambulated 20 feet with walker and minimum assistance of 1”, state “Patient advances walker excessively and demonstrates step-to-gait pattern.  Loss of balance x 1 on level requiring PT to correct.  Physical facilitation and verbal cuing for step-through pattern and walker placement.  Able to sustain correction x 20’ without loss of balance.”
  • Show a functional correlation to all therapeutic activities
  • Be specific regarding plan for next treatment/visit
  • Avoid documentation that reflects repetitive exercises and activities

 

Principle Two: Document why treatment is medically appropriate

  • Describe prior level of functional mobility and self-care
  • What event caused the change?
  • Describe the patient strengths that indicate potential to restore/improve function or safety
  • Goals should reflect a MATERIAL improvement in the patient’s ability to function more independently or more safely
  • Establish a home maintenance program and teach the patient or caregiver
Principle Three: Treatment is dependent on patient's condition
  • Document any other conditions that may support need for therapy in situations that would not ordinarily need a therapist.
  • Example: A patient needs ongoing range of motion on left leg.  Often a home health aide or family member could provide this after being taught.  This patient needs the therapist to do the range of motion because they have a non-healing fracture.

Use your Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC) guidelines.

  • The following Regional Home Health and Hospice Intermediaries (RHHIs) have Local Coverage Determinations (LCDs) for therapy:
  • CGS: Physical Therapy for Home Health
  • NGS: No active policies
  • Palmetto GBA: Physical Therapy for Home Health, Occupational Therapy for Home Health,  Home Health Speech-Language Pathology, and Home Health Surface Electrical Stimulation in the Treatment of Dysphagia. PT & OT: Requires that short and long term goals are stated in objective measurable terms and that there be an expected date of accomplishment.
  • The physical therapy LCDs utilize billing codes to categorize interventions and modalities.  These codes are not used in home health, but most therapists are familiar with these codes and use them.
  • It should also be noted that some LCDs give ranges of visits for modalities.  These ranges are guidelines, and not rules.  The LCD also provides additional documentation recommendations.  For example, CGS’s Physical Therapy for Home Health LCD states for Gait Training, “Generally not covered for greater than 12 – 18 visits within a 4 – 6-week period.”  Documentation must support the need for continued treatment beyond this frequency and duration.
