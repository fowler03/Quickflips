Face-to-Face Encounter/Certification

Face-to-Face (FTF)

Patients admitted to home health under Medicare, and most Medicaid programs, must have been seen by a physician or a non-physician practitioner (i.e., Nurse Practitioner or Physician Assistant) within the last 90 days prior to the home health admission, or within 30 days after admission.  This Face-to-Face (FTF) visit must be for the same reason for which home health is seeing the patient. 

 

Helpful hints to meet this requirement are:
  • Ask upon referral if the patient has been seen by a physician recently.  This may have been in the hospital or subacute stay.
  • Request discharge summary or visit documentation.  This encounter documentation is required.
  • The visit documentation must contain the following components:
  • Allowed provider type: Physician or non-physician practitioner’s name and credentials
  • Date of encounter, which falls within the 90 day prior or 30 day after SOC window
  • The visit must reflect the reason for the encounter, which must also be the reason for home health care

 

The visit should also contain clinical indications that supports the medical necessity of home health care and the taxing effort it takes for the patient to leave the home.  If this is not clear, or strongly documented, the home health agency may corroborate this information for the physician by providing documentation that support these qualifications for home health, based on the initial assessment findings.

  • Upon admission, if a patient has not had a recent FTF for the same reason as the home health services, assist the patient in setting up an appointment.  Follow up to ensure this has occurred and is essential for compliance.
  • Assist in facilitating transportation to the appointment (i.e. family member).
Face to Face and Certification

The certification is an attestation that a face to face encounter occurred, and that home health is medically necessary, and that the patient is homebound.  The certification must be signed and dated by a physician, MD, DO or DPM when appropriate. 

 

The FTF and certification may be done in conjunction with the plan of care in five different ways:
  1. The same physician, who saw the patient may provide the documentation of the FTF encounter, sign the certification and the plan of care.
  2. The inpatient/subacute physician performs the FTF encounter, provides documentation of the certification, hands off to the community physician to follow the patient, and signs POC.
  3. The inpatient/subacute physician performs the FTF and documents the FTF clinical findings.  The community physician uses theclinical findingsfrom the inpatient visit to certify that the encounter occurred and, signs the POC.A non-physician practitioner (NPP), under the supervision of the community physician may complete the FTF.  The physician performs the certification and the POC.
  4. A NPP, under supervision of the physician in the acute or subacute setting performs the FTF, then hands off to the facility physician.  The facility physician can both document the certification and attest the FTF occurred (as in #2 above), or communicate the FTF findings to the community physician for that physician to perform both the certification and the POC (as in #3 above).

 

If an agency finds there is a lack of clinical indicators for medical necessity for home health, or homebound status, the agency can provide additional information for the physician to corroborate the documentation of the visit.  This is true in any of the scenarios above.  The physician is to maintain a copy of the corroborating documentation for his/her records for the patient.

 

The corroborating documentation can be provided in any format that the agency chooses.

 

Each agencyneeds to have processes in place to ensure that documentation needed for both FTF and certifications is completed within required time frames.
