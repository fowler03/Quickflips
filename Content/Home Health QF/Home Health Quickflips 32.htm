Staging Pressure Ulcers

 

When assessing a pressure ulcer, clinicians are expected to “stage” the ulcer for an accurate description and implied standards for treatment.  Pressure ulcers are identified with Arabic numbers in OASIS and literature.

Stage 1 Pressure Ulcer:

  • Skin intact, no open area
  • Non-blanchable erythema
  • On darker skin, a Stage I may present as discoloration, an area of warmth and edema, or firmness
  • Would be included in care-planning, but would not alone warrant skills of nurse for care

Stage 2 Pressure Ulcer:

  • Area of partial thickness skin loss
  • Superficial skin loss with red-pink wound bed, no slough
  • Abrasion, shallow crater, intact, or blister
  • Very common in home health
  • Be sure to document what requires the skills of a nurse

Stage 3 Pressure Ulcer:

  • Deep crater (with or without undermining of adjacent tissue)
  • Full-thickness skin loss involving damage or necrosis of subcutaneous tissue
  • Extends down to, but not through, underlying fascia
  • Fat may be visible but bone, tendon, or muscle not exposed
  • Slough may be present
  • May include undermining and tunneling

Stage 4 Pressure Ulcer:

  • Full-thickness skin loss with visible bone, tendon, or muscle
  • Extensive tissue destruction and necrosis
  • Slough or eschar may be present
  • Often includes undermining and tunneling

 

Suspected Deep Tissue Injury in Evolution:

  • Purple or maroon localized area of intact skin
  • May present as a blood-filled blister
  • Preceded by tissue that is painful, firm, mushy, boggy, warmer or cooler than surrounding skin
  • May evolve rapidly, exposing more tissue layers even with optimal treatment
  • May be difficult to detect in dark skin

 

Unstageable:

  • Full thickness tissue loss in which the base is covered by slough and/or eschar in the wound bed
  • Until enough slough and/or eschar is removed to expose base of the wound, the depth and therefore stage cannot be determined

 

Important to remember:

  1. Patient assessments should be completed as close to the actual time of the SOC/ROC as possible.
  2. Do not change assessment for an ulcer that increases in numerical stage (worsens) within the assessment time period
  3. Changing information in the 5-day window for pressure ulcers is no longer valid. Report as close to admission visit as possible.
