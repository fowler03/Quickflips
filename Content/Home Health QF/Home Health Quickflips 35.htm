M1334: Problematic Stasis Ulcer Status

 

This M item asks for a description of the status of the most problematic observable stasis ulcer. 

To determine the most problematic ulcer, use clinical judgment and consider:

  • History of ulcers
  • Drainage (amount, color and odor)
  • Locations and size
  • Surrounding tissue
  • Contributing or exacerbating circumstances

If there is only one stasis ulcer, that one is the most problematic.

Do not consider stasis ulcers that are covered with a non-removable dressing or device (most commonly an Unna Boot) for this item.

To determine the healing status of the most problematic stasis ulcer, use the WOCN* guidelines on healing of stasis ulcers:

  • Newly epithelialized: wound bed completely covered with new epithelium, no exudate, no avascular tissue (eschar and/or slough), no signs or symptoms of infection. (All descriptors must be TRUE)  *This response will NEVER be used to report the healing status of a stasis ulcer since once a stasis ulcer completely epithelializes it is no longer reported
  • Fully granulating:  wound bed filled with granulation tissue to the level of surrounding skin, no dead space, no avascular tissue (eschar and/or slough), no signs or symptoms of infection, and wound edges open. (All descriptors must be TRUE)
  • Early/partial granulation:  >25% or more covered with granulation tissue, <25% of the wound bed is covered with necrotic or avascular tissue, no signs or symptoms of infection, and wound edges open. (All descriptors must be TRUE)
  • Not-healing:  > 25% avascular tissue (eschar and/or slough) or signs/symptoms of infection or clean but non-granulating wound bed or closed/hyperkeratotic wound edges or hypergranulation or a persistent failure to improve despite appropriate comprehensive wound management. (If any one applies status is "not healing")

Note: This M item affects both the clinical domain reimbursement and the non-routine supplies reimbursement.

 

*The Wound, Ostomy and Continence Nurse’s (WOCN) have published OASIS guidelines on assessing stasis ulcers.  For more information, go to www.wocn.org.
