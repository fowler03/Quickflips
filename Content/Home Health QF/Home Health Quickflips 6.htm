Four Principals of Skilled Nursing

 

There are four principles to use when making a determination if skilled nursing services are covered by Medicare.  Review these principles at start of care and throughout the episode of care.  Recertification is an important milestone to thoroughly reassess if the patient is still eligible under Medicare.  All of the principles should be considered when making this determination, and Principle Four must be met in all circumstances.

 

Principle One: Requires the skills of a nurse
  1. This principle is the most straightforward.  Ask yourself, “Do the services I am providing require the special training of a nurse?”
  2. Provision of specific services, such as: injections, catheter changes, complex wound care, observation and assessment, or teaching.
  3. Please see your state’s Nurse Practice Act for skills that an RN must perform and those that LVN/LPN can perform under the supervision of an RN.
  4. Remember, meeting this principle alone does not indicate Medicare coverage. 
Principle Two: Service is not skilled just because it’s performed by a nurse
  • As part of a visit, the nurse will perform clinical tests or procedures that a patient, family member or home health aide could perform.  These services alone would not constitute a skilled visit by themselves – skilled services must also be performed during the visit. 
  • Examples of “non-skilled” services include: glucometer checks, simple wound care, blood pressure check.
  • The patient or caregiver’s inability to perform these tasks does not change the fact that the service is not skilled.

 

Principle Three: Service does not become unskilled because it has been taught
  • Caregivers can be taught skilled procedures in the home, such as: complex wound care, injections, IVs, etc. These services remain “skilled”, even if a caregiver is performing them.
  • If the caregiver becomes unwilling or unavailable to provide the services, these services remain skilled and a nurse can still perform the services as needed.

 

Principle Four: Reasonable and necessary
  • Patient condition warrants the care.
  • Non-skilled services could require the skills of a nurse based on the patient’s medical condition, e.g. cleansing a superficial wound and applying topical ointment would not necessarily require the skills of a nurse.  But, if this uncomplicated wound is on the foot of a diabetic patient, skilled nursing may be reasonable and medically necessary.
  • Medical necessity must be evident in documentation.  Be sure to include:
  • Objective terms regarding service performed
  • Description of patient condition, including comorbidities that affect care
  • Patient’s response (or lack thereof) to treatment
  • Changes in condition
  • Changes in treatment
  • Communication with physician and other disciplines
  • Instruction provided

 

For further information, see the Medicare Benefit Policy Manual, CMS Publication 100-2, Chapter 7, Section 40.1
