Administering Injectable Medication

 

Administering medications by injection or intravenously requires the skills of a nurse.  Ask these questions to determine whether this medication route is reasonable for the patient:

  • What is the medication used for?
  • Look in the Physician’s Desk Reference (PDR).
  • Look in a nurse’s drug guide.
  • Search pharmaceutical websites.
  • Contact pharmacy.
  • Determine proper diagnosis, and if any other treatments should be tried first.
  • Does the patient have a diagnosis for which this medication is prescribed?
  • If other treatments have been tried first, is there documentation of this?
  • Contact physician and ask why this medication was chosen, and document the response.
  • Does this medication come in another, less invasive form?
  • Oral, topical, sublingual, suppository or inhaled
  • If so, we need documentation why patient needs the injectable form.
  • Need should be patient specific – not an overall statement about the efficacy of injection versus oral.
  • Need could be due to past non-compliance, or side-effects.
  • Is this a subcutaneous medication that is generally accepted as a “self -administered medication”?
  • Insulin, Enbrel, Heparin, and Calcimar are common examples.
  • See your MAC’s website for others (Please refer to “Where to Find the Rules” Quickflip© #57).
  • SN can provide a medication that is generally considered “self-administered,” IF the following is documented:
  • The reason the patient is unable to administer the medication (e.g. poor vision, dementia, tremors affecting fine motor function)
  • There is no willing or able caregiver to administer the medication
