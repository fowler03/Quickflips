Assessment and Care Planning

The comprehensive assessment and Plan of Care (POC) are mirrors of each other.  The POC must be individualized, based on the initial and ongoing comprehensive assessment of the patient.  Assessment and care planning are ongoing, continuous processes updated by the interdisciplinary team (IDT), as the patient’s needs and condition changes.  Use the following lists to ensure that your assessment and POC are complete.

Assessment should include:

  • Physical, psychosocial, emotional and spiritual needs
  • Nature and condition of terminal illness
  • Complications and risk factors
  • Functional status
  • Severity of symptoms
  • Patient response to hospice interventions
  • Drug profile
  • Bereavement needs
  • Imminence of death
  • Referrals
  • Data elements that allow for measurement of outcomes

Plan of Care should include:

  • Patient and family goals
  • Interventions for problems identified through assessment process
  • All services for palliation and management of terminal illness and related conditions
  • Drugs and treatments
  • Scope and frequency of services
  • Medical supplies and appliances
  • Measurable outcomes
  • Coordination with other providers
  • Documentation of patient/family understanding and agreement with the POC
  • Progress towards outcomes and goals

 

Hospice Item Set (HIS) and Impact on Assessment and Care Planning
  • Beginning July 2014, CMS mandated that hospices report quality data utilizing the Hospice Item Set (HIS) as the data collection tool
  • Seven (7) quality measures  chosen by CMS are seen as “best practices” which are a reflection of the level of quality care provided
  • HIS data will be pulled from the IDT’s assessments and interventions in the POC
  • Timeliness in the completion of assessment and POC is essential to meet the CMS mandated timeframes (See Quickflip #19 for Hospice Dates and Times)
  • See Quickflip #16a for Hospice Item Set
 Follow these steps for a dynamic, continuous assessment and care planning process.
  1. Patients are assessed by each discipline.  Problems, goals and interventions are identified (based on the patient’s/family’s needs and goals).  This group of individualized problems/goals/interventions is collectively called the Plan of Care (POC).
  2. Based on the POC, each discipline provides care/service to the patient/family.  That means that each visit should be focused on a problem identified on the POC, and reflect professional intervention(s) to achieve the desired goal.
  3. Document the patient’s progress toward meeting the desired goal(s) in the POC after each visit.  The Interdisciplinary Team (IDT) summarizes the effectiveness of interventions and plans for the next two weeks on the IDT summary form.  Every visit note and IDT note should reflect attention to the problems/goals/interventions.
  4. If you are seeing a patient, and the focus of the visit is not on one of the problems identified in the POC, ask “Am I focused on a new problem?”  If so, add it to the POC!  If you are not focused on a new or existing problem, ask “Is there a need for me to visit?”
  5. If you are seeing a patient and none of the problems on the POC requires you or the patient’s attention, ask yourself, “Are these problems resolved?”  If so, document the resolution.
  6. If there are consistently no problems that you and the patient/family can work to resolve together, ask yourself “Am I seeing this patient too frequently?  Does this patient require intervention from my discipline at this time?”  If you are a case manager, ask “Is this patient still eligible for hospice care?”
  7. Adjust visit frequencies for the upcoming two weeks based on patient/family needs and goals.
