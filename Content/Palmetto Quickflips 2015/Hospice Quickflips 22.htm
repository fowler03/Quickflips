IDT Meeting “Do’s” and “Don’ts”

Please DO!
  • Come to IDT meeting knowing the care plan for all your patients, the patient’s response to the care plan interventions and whether any interventions need to be changed.
  • Talk to your teammates! Leave voicemail messages, e-mail, and read each other’s progress notes between IDT meetings.  Document that you communicated with them!  The meeting is not meant to take the place of interdisciplinary communication.
  • Gently redirect your colleagues if they go off on tangents during the meeting.
  • Use staff support and other “together” times to share your triumphs, tears and challenges.  That way you won’t feel “cut off” when redirected during IDT meeting.
  • Be concise and stick to the agreed upon presentation format when reporting on patients.  This leaves time for in-depth discussion of patients who require it.
  • Document “Well managed with current interventions” or “Well managed with the current Plan of Care” instead of “No change.”  This simply means that the patient is being comfortably maintained due to your interventions.  All your efforts and professional interventions are documented in your progress/visit notes.  Document that patient comfort is being maintained successfully with the current clinical regimen.

 

Please DO NOT!
  • Come to IDT meeting without knowing which problems you are working on with your patients.
  • Tell the story of your last visit.
  • Save all your communication for the IDT meeting.  It’s just one meeting, and it’s not intended to be a substitute for regular interdisciplinary communication and collaboration.

 

Who Should Attend IDT
  • Required: MD, RN, social services, spiritual care services
  • Recommended:
  • Hospice aides – often have the most pertinent information about the patient’s condition, especially for recertification discussions
  • Admission nurses – so they can understand the rest of the care process (rotating)
  • Any other staff in contact with patients

 

Role of MD in IDT
  • Participate in the care discussions as appropriate – avoid using this time to “catch up on charting”
  • Provide orders for care changes decided upon in the meeting
  • Problem solve as a part of the team
  • Listen to other team members to learn more about the patients

 

Recertification Presentations by RN
  • Prepare ahead – be ready to present the patient’s pertinent data to support your belief that the patient should or should not be recertified
  • Use the last benefit period to frame your presentation – what has changed during the last benefit period?
  • Don’t present a snapshot of the patient’s current condition without comparison to their prior status
  • Suggested format: “The patient is an X year old man/woman with Y diagnosis who has declined in the past benefit period as evidenced by the following...”

 

For Manager Running The Meeting
  • Solicit input from all disciplines on each patient
  • RN, MSW, MD, spiritual care, hospice aide
