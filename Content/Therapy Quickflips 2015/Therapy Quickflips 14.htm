Home Exercise Programs

Instruction in Home Exercise Programs (HEP) is a key intervention to promote functional improvement and encourage increased patient participation in their care.

 

Initial Assessment to Identify

  • Pertinent medical problems needing HEP
  • Functional goal from HEP implementation
  • Physiological response to exercise

 

Documentation Elements
  • What exercises were taught and why
  • Repetitions and frequency of exercises
  • When and how much to increase
  • Precautions and when to stop
  • Specifics on difference between Ther Ex provided during visit vs HEP instruction
  • Patient/caregiver’s response to exercises to include repeat demo, verbal and tactile cues
  • Physician orders reflect type of HEP to be provided (for example, HEP for BLE strengthening and balance; RUE NMRe-ed and AAROM)
  • Limit use of abbreviations
  • Copy of instructions uploaded/provided for medical records
  • Handouts clearly identified with notation of specific instructions for patient

 

Example: New HEP instruction for weight bearing (WB) and functional return of hemiplegic LUE to increase ability to get out of bed. Instructed pt. and caregiver in WB side-lying position sitting at edge of bed with elbow flexed and progress to WB with elbow extended. Instruct to perform each exercise daily, 5 reps each holding 5 counts, increase to 10 reps and 10 counts as tolerated, to stop with any new or exacerbation of pain. Pt. able to repeat demo with min A without report of pain x 5 reps, holding 5 counts. Caregiver required 50% cueing to support elbow extension and pt.’s correct hand placement. Pt. & caregiver agreed to do new exercises.

 

Assessment and Documentation in Subsequent Visits
  • Demonstrate patient’s progress and adherence to prior instruction
  • Show exercise instruction is skilled and not repetitive
  • State what new or upgraded instruction and/or verbal/tactile cues were needed
  • State how much caregiver assistance was needed
  • State which instructions were given to caregiver

 

Patient Compliance Strategies
  • Clear instructions to patient and caregivers in lay terms
  • Obtain agreements to participate in program (verbal or written)
  • Use calendars and pre-set time of day to perform the activity (for example, 30 minutes after each meal, every MWF at 10 am and 2 pm)
  • Develop charts and flow sheets to record compliance or progression of program
  • Suggest exercises with companion or listening to music, etc.
  • Encourage rewards as each goal is reached
  • Remember: Habits are formed if done for at least 21 days
